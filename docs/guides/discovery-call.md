# Discovery Call Guide for Arc Systems

**Purpose:** Qualify leads, assess mutual fit, demonstrate expertise WITHOUT giving away implementation details.

**Duration:** 30-45 minutes maximum

**Goal:** Client leaves thinking "This is more complex than I thought. I need this person." ‚Äî NOT "Great, now I can build it myself."

---

## üéØ MINDSET BEFORE THE CALL

### You Are NOT:
- ‚ùå A free consultant
- ‚ùå A tech stack recommender
- ‚ùå An architecture diagram service
- ‚ùå Desperate for any project

### You ARE:
- ‚úÖ Assessing if they're a good fit for YOU
- ‚úÖ Understanding their business problem (not designing the solution)
- ‚úÖ Demonstrating depth without revealing specifics
- ‚úÖ Surfacing complexity they haven't considered
- ‚úÖ Creating dependency on your expertise

## üó£Ô∏è CALL STRUCTURE

### 1. OPENING (2-3 minutes)

**What to say:**
> "Thanks for taking the time to connect. I've got about 30-45 minutes blocked. Before we dive in, I'd love to understand your business and what's driving this project. Then I can share whether we're a good fit and what next steps might look like. Sound good?"

**Why this works:**
- Sets time boundary (implies you're busy/valuable)
- Frames it as mutual assessment ("if we're a good fit")
- You're in control

---

### 2. UNDERSTAND THEIR BUSINESS (5-7 minutes)

**ASK (Business Context):**
- "Tell me about your business. What do you do?"
- "Who are your customers?"
- "What's working well right now?"
- "What's the biggest bottleneck or challenge you're facing?"

**DON'T:**
- ‚ùå Jump to technical questions
- ‚ùå Interrupt with solutions
- ‚ùå Mention any technologies yet

**Why this matters:**
- Most developers skip this ‚Äî you'll stand out
- Uncovers their REAL problem (often different from what they think)
- Shows you care about business outcomes, not just code

---

### 3. UNDERSTAND THE PROJECT (8-10 minutes)

**ASK (Problem Understanding):**
- "What made you decide now is the time to build this?"
- "Have you tried solving this problem before? What happened?"
- "If this project succeeds, what changes for your business?"
- "What does success look like 6 months after launch?"
- "Who are the key users? What's their technical comfort level?"

**ASK (Constraints):**
- "What's your timeline? Is there a specific deadline driving this?"
- "What's your budget range for this? (Ballpark is fine)"
- "Do you have an internal team that will work on this post-launch?"
- "Any technical constraints we should know about? (existing systems, compliance, etc.)"

**DON'T SAY:**
- ‚ùå "You should use Next.js with PostgreSQL"
- ‚ùå "We'll build this with React and Node.js"
- ‚ùå "Here's the architecture..." [draws diagram]
- ‚ùå "It'll take X weeks and cost $X" (too specific)

**DO SAY (Demonstrate Depth Without Specifics):**
- ‚úÖ "There are several architectural approaches for this, each with tradeoffs around scalability, cost, and speed."
- ‚úÖ "This involves data pipeline considerations, real-time processing challenges, and integration complexity."
- ‚úÖ "I'm seeing 3-4 different technical paths depending on your long-term roadmap."
- ‚úÖ "The ML component here requires careful consideration of training data, model selection, and inference optimization."

**Surface Complexity They Haven't Considered:**
- ‚úÖ "Have you thought about data sovereignty requirements for UAE?"
- ‚úÖ "How are you planning to handle user authentication and role-based access?"
- ‚úÖ "What's your disaster recovery strategy?"
- ‚úÖ "At scale, this introduces caching and performance optimization needs."
- ‚úÖ "Integration with [system they mentioned] typically involves OAuth flows and webhook management."

**Why this works:**
- Shows you've done this before (pattern recognition)
- Reveals gaps in their thinking
- Makes them realize: "Oh, this is more complex than I thought"
- Creates dependency: "I need someone who understands all this"

---

### 4. ASSESS FIT (5-7 minutes)

**RED FLAGS (Walk away if):**
- Budget is unrealistic (<$5k for complex AI project)
- Timeline is impossible ("Need it in 2 weeks")
- They've burned through multiple developers
- They want you to sign an NDA before discussing anything
- They're asking for free proof of concept
- "We'll pay you in equity" (unless you're interested)
- They use phrases like "quick and easy" or "it's just like Uber but for..."
- They want you to bid against 10 other developers

**GREEN FLAGS (Good fit):**
- Clear business problem
- Reasonable budget and timeline
- Previous attempts show learning
- Respect your expertise
- Ask smart questions
- Understand this is a partnership

**What to say if it's NOT a fit:**
> "Thanks for sharing all this. To be honest, based on what you've described, I don't think we're the right fit. [Reason: timeline, budget, expertise]. I'd recommend [honest advice, maybe refer competitor]. But I appreciate you thinking of Arc Systems."

**Why being honest works:**
- Saves your time
- Builds reputation (honest advisor)
- They might come back later with better project
- Referrals from grateful people you turned down

---

### 5. OUTLINE NEXT STEPS (3-5 minutes)

**If it's a GOOD fit, say:**
> "This sounds like a great fit for what we do. Here's how we'd move forward:
> 
> 1. I'll put together a **detailed technical proposal** that outlines:
>    - Our recommended architecture and technical approach
>    - Implementation roadmap with phases
>    - Timeline breakdown
>    - Transparent pricing with line-item breakdown
>    - Team structure and communication plan
> 
> 2. The proposal takes 2-3 business days to prepare because we do proper technical analysis.
> 
> 3. Once you review it, we can schedule another call to walk through the proposal, answer questions, and adjust if needed.
> 
> 4. If we're aligned, we move to contracts (MSA and SOW) and kick off.
> 
> Sound good?"

**What NOT to say:**
- ‚ùå "Let me send you a quote" (too vague)
- ‚ùå "It'll cost around $X" (never quote on first call)
- ‚ùå "I can start next week" (seems desperate)

---

### 6. HANDLE COMMON OBJECTIONS

**"Can you give me a ballpark price now?"**
> "I could throw out a number, but it wouldn't be accurate without proper analysis. Every project has nuances. I'd rather give you a detailed, honest quote in the proposal than a guess that's off by 50%. Fair?"

**"Can you just tell me what tech stack to use?"**
> "Absolutely, and that'll be in the proposal along with the rationale. The right stack depends on your specific constraints, team capabilities, and growth plans ‚Äî I need to think through those tradeoffs carefully."

**"We're talking to other developers. What makes you different?"**
> "Smart approach. Here's what clients tell us: We combine deep AI/ML expertise with full-stack capabilities, so you're not coordinating multiple vendors. We're senior-level on every project ‚Äî no juniors learning on your dime. And our proposals are detailed technical documents, not sales pitches. You'll know exactly what you're getting."

**"Can you build a proof of concept first?"**
> "POCs are great for validating technical feasibility. If that's needed, we'd scope that as a standalone paid engagement (typically 1-2 weeks, $X-$Y range). Then you own that code and can decide whether to continue with full development. Make sense?"

**"This is urgent. Can you start immediately?"**
> "I understand the urgency. The fastest path is actually starting with proper planning. Jumping in without a clear technical plan costs more time and money later. We can prioritize the proposal and turn it around in 1-2 business days. If approved, we can start within a week."

## üöÄ ADVANCED TECHNIQUES

### The "Layered Complexity" Technique
When they describe a "simple" feature:
> "That's the user-facing simplicity, which is great. Behind that, there's [authentication, data validation, error handling, edge cases, security, performance optimization]. The craft is making complexity feel simple."

### The "Strategic Questions" Technique
Make them think deeper:
> "Have you considered what happens when [edge case]?"
> "How do you want to handle [scenario they didn't mention]?"
> "What's your strategy for [growth/scaling concern]?"

This positions you as the strategic thinker and reveals gaps in their planning.

### The "Options Framework" Technique
Never give ONE answer:
> "There are typically 3 approaches: Quick MVP, Balanced, or Enterprise-grade. Each has tradeoffs in cost, timeline, and scalability. In the proposal, I'll recommend one based on your constraints and explain why."

Shows you're thoughtful, not prescriptive.

## üíº WHEN TO WALK AWAY

Trust your gut. Walk away if:
- They treat you like a code monkey, not a partner
- Budget is <$10k for complex AI work
- They're disrespectful of your time
- They want you to work for free first
- They've burned 3+ developers before you
- They want to "test" you with unpaid work
- The project requires expertise you don't have (and can't learn quickly)
- Something feels off

**Remember:** Saying no to bad clients creates space for great clients.
